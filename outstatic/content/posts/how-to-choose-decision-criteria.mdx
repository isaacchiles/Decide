Criteria are the quiet, load-bearing part of any [weighted decision matrix](/blog/weighted-decision-matrix-guide). Get them right and the rest of the decision mostly takes care of itself. Get them wrong and you'll do a lot of careful-looking math that confirms a conclusion you reached on a whim. So let's get them right.

## What are decision criteria?

Decision criteria are the factors you judge your options against — the columns in your matrix. If you're choosing a job, your criteria might be salary, growth, commute, and how much you'd enjoy the work. If you're choosing a laptop, they might be performance, price, battery, and weight. Criteria are *what matters*; the scores are *how each option does on what matters*; and the weights (a separate step) are *how much each one counts*.

## Why you should choose your criteria before you look at the options

This is the rule that does the heavy lifting, so it's worth saying plainly: decide what matters about the *kind* of decision before you start judging the actual options.

Here's the trap if you don't. The moment you have a favorite — the apartment with the great light, the job with the famous logo — your brain quietly goes to work building a case for it. Ask yourself for criteria *after* that, and you'll generate the exact list that happens to make your favorite win. It feels like rigor. It's actually a rationalization wearing rigor's clothes, and the tidy matrix at the end gives a gut call a lab coat.

Choosing criteria first breaks the spell. When you write down "what makes a good apartment for me" before you've toured anything, you're being honest, because you have nothing yet to defend. Then the options have to earn their scores against a standard you set with a clear head.

## How many criteria should you use?

Five to eight is the sweet spot. Fewer than five and you're probably leaving out something that'll matter later. More than eight and two things go wrong: the list gets exhausting to score, and — more importantly — the weights get spread so thin that nothing carries real signal. If everything's a factor, nothing's a deciding factor. When your list balloons, group the small stuff (merge "cup holders" and "trunk space" into "practicality") and cut anything you wouldn't actually change your mind over.

## What makes a good criterion?

Three tests. A good criterion is **distinct**, **directional**, and **durable**.

*Distinct* means it doesn't secretly overlap another one. *Directional* means higher is always better, so your scores point the same way. *Durable* means you'll still care about it after the new-thing excitement fades.

| Weak criterion | Why it's weak | Stronger version |
|---|---|---|
| "Value" | Smuggles price and quality into one blurry score | Split into "Price" and "Quality" |
| "Not too expensive" | Higher score is ambiguous — is 8 cheap or pricey? | "Affordability" (higher = easier on the budget) |
| "Vibes" | Real, but too vague to score consistently | "How comfortable I'd feel here day to day" |
| "Good brand" | A proxy for things you actually care about | The underlying criteria: reliability, support, resale |

## How to find the criteria you're forgetting

The dangerous criteria are the ones you leave off entirely, because a matrix can only weigh what you put in it. Three quick ways to surface them:

**The regret test.** Picture yourself six months in, unhappy with your choice. What went wrong? Whatever you imagine is a criterion you care about — add it.

**The "what would make me say no?" test.** For each option, finish the sentence "I'd walk away if…". Those dealbreakers are criteria, and often the most important ones.

**The future-you test.** Ask which factors matter to the version of you who has to *live with* this for years, not the version who's excited about it today. Excitement over-weights the shiny; future-you remembers the commute.

## Criteria vs. weights — what's the difference?

Criteria are *what* matters; weights are *how much*. They're two separate steps on purpose, and conflating them is a common mistake. First build an honest, complete list of criteria with a clear head. *Then*, in a separate pass, decide how much each one counts. Doing them together tempts you to drop a criterion just because it's inconvenient for your favorite — keep it on the list and let the weight, not the omission, reflect its importance.

## Common mistakes (and the fixes)

The usual ones, all avoidable. Reverse-engineering criteria to fit a favorite (fix: choose them first). Listing the same thing three times under different names (fix: the distinctness test). Mixing directions so some scores mean "more is better" and others don't (fix: rephrase every criterion so higher = better). And forgetting the unglamorous factors that decide how you'll actually feel later (fix: run the regret and future-you tests).
